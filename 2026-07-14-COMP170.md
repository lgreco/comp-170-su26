## Why Programs Crash on Bad Input

We looked at two common mistakes: trying to divide a piece of text by a number, and subtracting a typed birth year from the current year without converting it first. Anything typed at `input()` comes in as text, even if it looks like a number, so `2026 - "1967"` fails the same way `2026 - "hello there"` would. The fix is to convert the text to a whole number with `int()` before doing arithmetic on it — but `int()` itself fails if the text isn't made of plain digits (Roman numerals or a typo like `196Y` won't convert).

## Asking Until You Get a Good Answer

Rather than let the program crash the first time someone types something unusable, we talked about looping: keep asking the same question until the answer is usable. We also raised the bar past "any number will do" — a birth year like 900 might convert cleanly to an integer but would make someone 1,126 years old, so we still need a reasonableness check (no one born before 1901 or after 2025).

## Try, Except, and the Infinite-Retry Loop

We wrote a small `getYear` method with a loop that runs forever, wrapping the risky conversion in a `try` block and catching failures with `except ValueError`, printing "try again" and looping back instead of crashing. This pairing, called exception handling, lets a program recover from bad input gracefully instead of exposing an error message to the user. We'll add a limit (10 tries) and range checks tomorrow.
