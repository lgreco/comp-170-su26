## Introducing Lists (Arrays)

We started with a grounding scenario. With Omar's help, we imagined being the first employee at a tiny startup airline. The boss needs passenger names — so Omar walks down the line, asks each person, and comes back with a piece of paper listing the names in order. That piece of paper is the first intuition for what a list is in Python.

A list is a contiguous collection of items stored next to one another, accessible by position. We compared it to an apartment building where every unit is the same size: you know the starting room number, and you can reach any resident by counting doors. Contrast that with 100 people scattered across Manhattan in separate hotels — collecting them all takes forever. Arrays make data easy to find because everything lives together in a predictable layout.

## Zero-Based Indexing

We unpacked why Python (and almost every modern language) numbers positions starting from 0. With Andrew's help at an imaginary grocery checkout, we arrived at the key idea: your position in line equals how many people are in front of you. If no one is in front of you, you're position 0 — the first person. We also heard the historical story of IBM's 1954 donation of a supercomputer to MIT and how programmers learned that zero-based indexing made programs run faster on that hardware.

## Cumulative Algorithm — Computing an Average

With temperatures from Cheska's location in North Carolina (80, 88, 86, 89…), we compared two approaches. The first hard-codes the denominator; every new data point requires a manual edit, and forgetting that edit produces a wrong average. The second approach stores temperatures in a list and uses `len()` to compute the denominator automatically. The loop adds each temperature to a running total, and the average is the total divided by however many items are in the list — no manual updates needed. This pattern (initialize a sum, loop through the list, divide at the end) is called a **cumulative algorithm**.
