## Bella's question about nested loops

With Bella's help, we dug into a question from the week 7 solutions: how does a nested loop find one word hiding inside another? We started simple, printing a multiplication table using two loops — one loop running inside another. The outer loop picks a number 1 through 9, and for each one, the inner loop runs through 1 through 9 again to print all the products. We also looked at three loops nested together and counted how many times a line would print: if each loop runs 10 times, the innermost line prints $10^3$, or 1,000 times. We talked about where this shows up in the real world — sorting through rows and columns of a big table of numbers, or numbering every seat in a theater, row by row, seat by seat.

## Searching for a word inside another word

Then we tackled the real question: how do we find the letters "as" inside "bananas," and at what position? The idea is to scan the text from left to right looking for a spot where the current letter matches the first letter we're searching for. Every time we find one, we check the letters that come right after it to see if they match the rest of what we're looking for, one at a time.

We built this checking step as a running true-or-false answer: start by assuming we have a match, then for each next letter, ask "is this one the same, too?" and combine the answers with logical *and*. If every letter matches, we still have "true" at the end and we've found our word. The moment one letter doesn't match, the whole thing becomes "false," and we know that spot wasn't the right one, so the search moves on.
